A 1968 advertisement for Western Electric’s Picturephone

Source

In 1964, AT&T (Western Electric) and Bell Labs demonstrated their picturephone system at the New York World’s Fair.1 These picturephones – equipped with two-way video and audio – aimed to meet management and executive needs. Despite an aggressive ad campaign and continually reducing rates, the picturephone would remain largely unused throughout the late 1960s and 1970s. Market research performed by Western Electric found that there was wide interest in the technology; and yet, factors such as small screen sizes, graphics limitations, and – most importantly – a lack of applications prevented users from adopting the picturephone. In 1972, after an unsuccessful campaign to market the picturephone, all further developments of the device were halted.

Despite its commercial failure, the picturephone represents a significant turning point in the history of videoconferencing. Through market research, the industry had gathered extensive documentation on what consumers wanted, as well as their behaviors with the technology. As they discovered, the use of teleconferencing technologies would be shaped by users and their behavior, not the other way around.

A 1988 magazine article on Picturetel’s new teleconferencing system

Network World magazine1

Pictel Corporation (later renamed to Picturetel Corporation) was founded in 1984 by two MIT students and their professor. In its infancy, the team was focused on developing a codec, or “an algorithm for compressing and decompressing video images from capture to transmission and eventual display.”2 Their first video communications product – the C-2000 – reached the market in the summer of 1986. Their success in reducing costs and achieving market adoption demonstrated the potential for video communications technology, and from there, the industry took off.

Following their disruptive debut, Picturetel continued to improve their video and audio quality. In 1988, they introduced the C-3000, which utilized their new video compression algorithm. The C-3000 offered a better visual and auditory experience at a much lower cost than its competitors, further marking Picturetel as an industry leader.

Beyond their products, Picturetel was also a key contributor to video standards; namely, the H.320 standard which specified videoconferencing standards over ISDN networks. Picturetel’s influence and revenue continued to grow over the next decade.

A screen capture of CU-SeeMe, released in 1992

Source

Cu-SeeMe was a free desktop video conferencing system developed at Cornell University in 1992.1 Designed for both Macintosh and Windows, Cu-SeeMe aimed at reaching as many users as possible. The program was able to run on inexpensive computers with minimal network connectivity, allowing for fast, accessible, interpersonal communication.2 Cu-SeeMe also allowed users to private message each other through “talk windows” or “chat boxes,” a feature that addressed Picturephone-era concerns that a video call would be too awkward to use regularly.

Having been developed by university students, CU-SeeMe immediately appealed to university settings. A study on collaborative technologies in inter-university settings found a use case of CU-SeeMe that particularly fit professors’ needs: “faculty collaboration sessions or meetings with students where the interaction was brief and the purpose of the communication was conveyed mostly by voice.”3

Following the example of MIT and Picturetel, CU-SeeMe is another instance of videoconferencing being driven by university settings. As efforts to maximize academic productivity accelerated, so did efforts to improve video conferencing technologies.

Diagram from the user manual of the Cisco Telepresence system 3000, 2008

Source

The Cisco Telepresence System 3000 (CTS-3000) was a product released within a batch of Cisco telepresence products in 2006.1 Focused at emulating a truly immersive experience, the CTS-3000 utilized “three 65-inch plasma screens; multi-channel discrete, spatial audio with echo cancellation per channel; microphones; lighting; Ethernet and power ports in the table legs, and a professional-quality table that seats six on each side.”2

Developers at Cisco were committed to the idea of creating a lifelike video conferencing experience, such that their mantra became “it’s all about the experience.” Additional factors they emphasized in creating the CTS-3000 were ease of use and reliability. The CTS-3000 needed to be accessible and intuitive to all, and it needed to work time after time – without fail.

Just seven years after its release, the CTS-3000 was retired.3 While Cisco seemingly had its users in mind, adoption for the CTS-3000 was low, with most evidence of its existence today being constrained to prototypes, mock-ups, or diagrams from a user manual spanning 216 pages.4
