In 1964, AT&T (Western Electric) and Bell Labs demonstrated their picturephone system at the New York World’s Fair. These picturephones – equipped with two-way video and audio – aimed to meet management and executive needs. Despite an aggressive ad campaign and continually reducing rates, the picturephone would remain largely unused throughout the late 1960s and 1970s. Market research performed by Western Electric found that there was wide interest in the technology; and yet, factors such as small screen sizes, graphics limitations, and – most importantly – a lack of applications prevented users from adopting the picturephone. In 1972, after an unsuccessful campaign to market the picturephone, all further developments of the device were halted.

Despite its commercial failure, the picturephone represents a significant turning point in the history of videoconferencing. Through market research, the industry had gathered extensive documentation on what consumers wanted, as well as their behaviors with the technology. As they discovered, the use of teleconferencing technologies would be shaped by users and their behavior, not the other way around.
